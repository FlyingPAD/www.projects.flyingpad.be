Leap Year

▸ The Earth ( 🌍 ) takes ± 365.25 days to orbit the Sun.

Which is known as a solar year !

▸ However, a calendar year has 365 days ...

The remaining 0.25 days accumulate each year ...

So that after 4 years, we have an extra day

( 0.25 days x 4 = 1 day ).

▸ To compensate for this difference, we add a day to the month of February every 4 years, making it 29 days instead of 28.

▸ This is called a leap year.

▸ A year is a leap year if it is divisible by 4...

▸ However, if the year is divisible by 100,

it is not a leap year unless it is also divisible by 400.

▸ This rule exists because 🌍 takes about 365.2425 days to orbit the Sun, not exactly 365.25 days.

▸ If we only used the rule of being divisible by 4,

we would add too many extra days over centuries ( 0.0075 days per year ).

▸ By skipping leap years that are divisible by 100,

we correct this overcompensation.

▸ But we also need to account for the fact that we slightly undercorrect,

so years divisible by 400 are leap years to adjust accurately.

▸ This fine-tuning keeps our calendar in sync with 🌍 orbit over long periods.
